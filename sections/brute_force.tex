\lhead{1. Brute Force Pattern Matching}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{\Alph*.}]

\item \textbf{Write the best-case and worst-case time complexities of the Brute
Force algorithm. Explain both cases briefly.}

The Brute Force Pattern Matching algorithm is one of the simplest string
matching techniques. It compares the pattern with the text character by
character at every possible position until a match is found or the entire
text has been checked.

Let:
\begin{itemize}
\item {\bfseries n = Length of the text}
\item {\bfseries m = Length of the pattern}
\end{itemize}
\vspace{0.5\baselineskip}

{\large\bfseries Best-Case Time Complexity: $O(n)$}

The best case occurs when the first character of the pattern does not match
the current text character. In such situations, only one comparison is needed
at each position before moving to the next position.

{\bfseries Example:}\\
\begin{tabular}{@{}rl@{}}
Text = & "ABCDEFGH"\\
Pattern = & "PQR"
\end{tabular}

Since the first character itself mismatches at every position, the algorithm
performs approximately \textbf{n} comparisons.

Therefore, the best-case complexity is $O(n)$.
\vspace{0.5\baselineskip}

{\large\bfseries Worst-Case Time Complexity: $O(nm)$}

The worst case occurs when most characters match successfully, but a mismatch
happens near the end of the pattern. As a result, the algorithm repeatedly
performs many comparisons at different positions.

{\bfseries Example:}\\
\begin{tabular}{@{}rl@{}}
Text = & "CCCCCCCCCC"\\
Pattern = & "CCCCD"
\end{tabular}

At every position, several characters match before the final mismatch occurs.
This increases the total number of comparisons significantly.

Therefore, the worst-case complexity is $O(nm)$.

\item \textbf{Explain the step-by-step working of the algorithm using a
suitable example.}

Consider:\par
\begin{tabular}{@{}rl@{}}
\textbf{Text} = & \textbf{COMPUTERBOOK}\\
\textbf{Pattern} = & \textbf{BOOK}
\end{tabular}

\vspace{0.5\baselineskip}

\textbf{Step 1: Start Comparison}\\
The algorithm begins by comparing the pattern with the text from the first position.

\begin{center}
\begin{NiceTabular}{c|cccccccccccc}
Text & C & O & M & P & U & T & E & R & B & O & O & K\\
Pattern & B & O & O & K

($(1-2.north west)+(-2pt,2pt)$)
rectangle
($(2-2.south east)+(2pt,-2pt)$);
}\end{NiceTabular}
\end{center}

Since C $\neq$ B, the pattern is shifted one position to the right.
\newpage

\textbf{Step 2: Shift the Pattern}

\begin{center}
\begin{NiceTabular}{c|cccccccccccc}
Text & C & O & M & P & U & T & E & R & B & O & O & K\\
Pattern & & B & O & O & K
\CodeAfter
\tikz{
\draw[rounded corners, thick, draw=red]
($(1-3.north west)+(-2pt,2pt)$)
rectangle
($(2-3.south east)+(2pt,-2pt)$);
}\end{NiceTabular}
\end{center}

Again, the first character does not match.\\
The pattern is shifted further.

\textbf{Step 3: Continue Comparing}

The algorithm keeps moving the pattern one position at a time and compares the\\
characters. Whenever a mismatch occurs, the current comparison stops and the
pattern is shifted to the next position.

\textbf{Step 4: Successful Match}

After several shifts:

\begin{center}
\begin{NiceTabular}{c|cccccccccccc}
Text & C & O & M & P & U & T & E & R & B & O & O & K\\
Pattern & &&&&&&&& B & O & O & K
\CodeAfter
\tikz{
\draw[rounded corners, thick, draw=customgreen]
($(1-10.north west)+(-2pt,2pt)$)
rectangle
($(2-13.south east)+(2pt,-2pt)$);
}
\end{NiceTabular}
\end{center}

Now all characters match:\\
B = B $\checkmark$\\
O = O $\checkmark$\\
O = O $\checkmark$\\
K = K $\checkmark$

Since every character matches successfully, the pattern \textbf{BOOK} is found in the text.

\textbf{Result:} The pattern \textbf{BOOK} occurs starting at position \textbf{8} of the text.
